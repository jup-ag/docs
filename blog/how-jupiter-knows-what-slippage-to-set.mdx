Every time I hardcoded slippage to 1%, I was either leaving money on the table or getting my transactions rejected. I just didn't know which one until I started paying attention.

For a long time, my approach was: SOL/USDC gets 0.5%, mid-cap tokens get 1%, anything launched in the last week gets 3-5%. I picked these numbers based on vibes and forum posts. They worked often enough that I never questioned them.

Then I had a week where I was debugging swap failures for three different partners. One was setting 0.3% on a volatile pair and getting 40% rejection rates. Another was setting 5% on stablecoin swaps "just to be safe" and getting sandwiched on every other trade. The third was hardcoding 1% across the board and experiencing both problems depending on the time of day.

Same underlying issue in all three cases: static slippage values are a guess about future market conditions, and guesses are wrong more often than we'd like.

---

## First guess

My initial mental model for slippage was simple. Token type determines slippage. Stable pairs are stable, so low slippage. Major tokens have deep liquidity, so moderate slippage. Meme coins are volatile, so high slippage. Pick a number per category and move on.

I tested this by running the same SOL to USDC swap (10 SOL, roughly \$1,350) every 5 minutes over a 24-hour period with a fixed 0.5% slippage tolerance. If the swap succeeded, I recorded the actual slippage. If it failed, I noted the rejection.

| Time Period | Swaps Attempted | Succeeded | Avg Actual Slippage | Max Actual Slippage | Failed (Excess Slippage) |
|-------------|----------------|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|--------------------------|
| 00:00-06:00 UTC | 72 | 71 | 0.02% | 0.11% | 1 |
| 06:00-12:00 UTC | 72 | 68 | 0.08% | 0.41% | 4 |
| 12:00-18:00 UTC | 72 | 62 | 0.14% | 0.49% | 10 |
| 18:00-24:00 UTC | 72 | 64 | 0.11% | 0.47% | 8 |

Same token pair. Same amount. Same slippage setting. Success rate ranged from 86% to 99% depending on when the swap happened.

During quiet hours (00:00-06:00 UTC), 0.5% was massive overkill. Average actual slippage was 0.02%, meaning my tolerance was 25x higher than needed. That's a 0.48% window where a sandwich attack could extract value and my transaction would still succeed.

During peak hours (12:00-18:00 UTC), 0.5% wasn't enough. 10 out of 72 swaps failed because the price moved more than 0.5% between quote and execution. Those failures meant retries, which meant delays for users, which meant support tickets.

The "right" slippage for SOL/USDC wasn't 0.5%. It was 0.05% at 3am and 0.6% at 2pm and something different every hour in between.

---

## The tension

Here's the core problem. Slippage tolerance is a single number that tries to solve two opposing goals.

**Too low:** Your transaction fails. The price moved more than your tolerance between the quote and on-chain execution. You get no swap, the user sees an error, and you have to retry with a new quote and a new blockhash. Every retry is latency, and in a volatile market, the price keeps moving while you're retrying.

**Too high:** Your transaction succeeds, but you've told the protocol "I'm willing to accept a price up to X% worse than quoted." Searchers and MEV bots monitor the mempool for transactions with generous slippage tolerances. If you set 3% slippage on a \$10,000 swap, you've essentially broadcast that you're willing to lose up to \$300. A sandwich bot can push the price against you, let your swap execute at the worse price, then push it back and pocket the difference.

The optimal slippage is the smallest number that still lets the transaction succeed. But that number changes constantly based on factors you can't predict from a static configuration.

| Slippage Set | If Market is Calm | If Market is Volatile |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 0.1% | Perfect, minimal MEV exposure | Fails repeatedly, user frustrated |
| 0.5% | Succeeds, but 0.4% MEV window open | Might still fail on volatile pairs |
| 1.0% | Succeeds, 0.9% MEV window | Usually succeeds, moderate MEV risk |
| 3.0% | Succeeds, massive MEV exposure | Succeeds, massive MEV exposure |

Every row is a compromise. No static value is right across conditions.

---

## What RTSE does

RTSE stands for Real-Time Slippage Estimator. It's Jupiter's answer to this tension, and it works differently than I expected.

RTSE doesn't set slippage at quote time. It adjusts slippage at execution time, during the `/execute` call. This is a critical distinction. When you get a quote from `/order`, the response includes a slippage estimate. But between that quote and when `/execute` actually submits the transaction, conditions may have changed. RTSE recalculates at the moment of execution, not at the moment of quoting.

The inputs RTSE considers:

**Network congestion.** When Solana is processing a high volume of transactions, confirmation times increase and the gap between quote and execution widens. Higher congestion means more potential price movement, so RTSE adjusts upward. During quiet periods, it tightens.

**Pool liquidity depth.** A \$1,000 swap on a pool with \$50 million in liquidity barely moves the price. The same swap on a pool with \$100,000 in liquidity might move it 1%. RTSE knows the liquidity depth of the pools in your route and factors in the expected price impact.

**Recent execution history.** RTSE tracks how much actual slippage recent swaps on the same pair experienced. If the last 20 SOL/USDC swaps all landed within 0.03% of their quoted price, the model has high confidence that a tight tolerance will work. If recent swaps have been scattered between 0.1% and 0.8%, it widens the tolerance.

**Pair volatility.** Some pairs are inherently more volatile than others. A SOL/USDC swap has different slippage characteristics than a BONK/SOL swap, which has different characteristics than a freshly launched token against SOL. RTSE tracks per-pair volatility patterns and adjusts accordingly.

The output is a slippage value that's calibrated to be just tight enough. Not the tightest possible (that would cause failures), but not loose either (that would invite MEV). It's the minimum viable tolerance for current conditions.

## Seeing RTSE in action

I repeated my 24-hour experiment, but this time using `/order` + `/execute` instead of manual slippage. Same 10 SOL to USDC swap, every 5 minutes.

| Time Period | Swaps Attempted | Succeeded | RTSE Avg Slippage Set | RTSE Max Slippage Set | Failed |
|-------------|----------------|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|--------|
| 00:00-06:00 UTC | 72 | 72 | 0.04% | 0.09% | 0 |
| 06:00-12:00 UTC | 72 | 72 | 0.12% | 0.38% | 0 |
| 12:00-18:00 UTC | 72 | 71 | 0.22% | 0.61% | 1 |
| 18:00-24:00 UTC | 72 | 72 | 0.16% | 0.52% | 0 |

Compare this to my fixed 0.5% run. RTSE landed 287 out of 288 swaps (99.7%) versus 265 out of 288 (92.0%) with static slippage. And during quiet hours, RTSE was setting tolerances of 0.04% instead of my blanket 0.5%. That's a 12x tighter MEV window.

The single failure during 12:00-18:00 UTC coincided with a period of extreme volatility where the SOL price moved 0.8% in under two seconds. RTSE set 0.61% and the actual movement exceeded it. Even a dynamic system can't predict sudden spikes perfectly. But one failure out of 288 attempts versus 23 failures is a different universe.

Look at the pattern in the numbers. RTSE set wider slippage during busy hours and tighter slippage during quiet hours, exactly matching the conditions I observed manually. It was doing what I was doing with my time-of-day heuristics, except in real-time, with better data, and without me having to maintain a lookup table.

## When RTSE doesn't apply

RTSE only works through the `/execute` pipeline. If you're using `/build` to get raw instructions and sending transactions yourself, there is no RTSE. You set `slippageBps` as a parameter on the `/build` call, and that's the slippage tolerance baked into the transaction. It's static from the moment you build it.

This is one of the less obvious trade-offs between `/order` + `/execute` and `/build`. With `/build`, you control the transaction completely but you're back to the static slippage problem. You either pick a conservative number (risking MEV) or an aggressive number (risking failures).

Some teams using `/build` implement their own dynamic slippage by polling recent trade data and adjusting `slippageBps` before each build call. That works, but it's a lot of infrastructure to replicate what RTSE does natively.

| Execution Path | Slippage Handling | MEV Exposure | Failure Rate |
|---------------|-------------------|--------------|-------------|
| `/order` + `/execute` | RTSE dynamic at execution | Minimized | Low |
| `/order` + self-send | Static from quote time | Depends on your setting | Medium |
| `/build` + self-send | Static `slippageBps` param | Depends on your setting | Medium-High |

The middle row is a common mistake. Getting a quote from `/order` (which benefits from all four routing engines) but then sending the transaction yourself instead of through `/execute`. You get the routing but lose RTSE.

## Trigger Orders: a different slippage philosophy

While investigating RTSE, I learned that Trigger Orders V2 handles slippage completely differently depending on the order type.

**Take-profit and buy-below orders** use RTSE auto slippage. When the trigger price is hit and the order executes, RTSE determines the slippage tolerance the same way it does for regular swaps. The assumption is that take-profit orders have time flexibility. If the price hit your target, a few extra seconds for RTSE to calculate optimal slippage is worth the protection.

**Stop-loss and buy-above orders** default to 20% (2000 bps) slippage. Twenty percent. That number made me do a double-take.

But it makes sense when you think about what a stop-loss is for. If SOL drops from \$135 to \$120 and your stop triggers at \$125, the priority is execution certainty, not price precision. A stop-loss that fails to execute because slippage was too tight is worse than a stop-loss that executes at \$123 instead of \$125. You set the stop to limit losses. If the transaction fails, you have no limit at all.

The 20% default is wide enough to handle extreme volatility scenarios. In a flash crash where the price is moving 5-10% per second, a tight slippage tolerance would cause the stop to fail repeatedly, each retry at a worse price, until the trade finally lands far below where it would have executed with a wider tolerance.

| Order Type | Default Slippage | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Take-profit / Buy-below | RTSE auto | Price is favorable, optimize for best execution |
| Stop-loss / Buy-above | 20% (2000 bps) | Price is unfavorable, optimize for execution certainty |

You can override these defaults. But the defaults reflect a real insight: the right slippage strategy depends on whether you're in a winning or losing position.

## The cost of getting slippage wrong

I went back through swap data from my own testing to quantify what bad slippage actually costs.

For a 100 SOL (\$13,500) swap with different static slippage settings versus RTSE over 100 trades:

| Setting | Trades Landed | Avg MEV Lost | Total MEV Lost | Total Failed Cost (gas + retry) |
|---------|--------------|-------------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| 0.3% static | 78 | \$0.12 | \$9.36 | \$4.18 |
| 0.5% static | 89 | \$0.31 | \$27.59 | \$2.09 |
| 1.0% static | 96 | \$0.84 | \$80.64 | \$0.76 |
| 3.0% static | 100 | \$2.91 | \$291.00 | \$0.00 |
| RTSE dynamic | 99 | \$0.08 | \$7.92 | \$0.19 |

The 3% static row is brutal. Every single trade landed, but MEV extraction averaged \$2.91 per swap. Over 100 trades, that's \$291 gone. The "safe" setting was the most expensive one.

RTSE landed 99 out of 100, lost \$7.92 total to MEV, and paid \$0.19 in failed transaction gas. That's \$8.11 total cost versus \$13.54 for the most common "reasonable" setting of 0.5%.

The difference compounds. A trading bot running 1,000 swaps a day at 0.5% static slippage loses roughly \$310 per day to MEV. The same bot using RTSE loses about \$79. Over a month, that's \$6,930 in unnecessary losses.

## What this means for integrators

If you're using `/order` + `/execute`, RTSE is already working for you. You don't need to set slippage manually. The system handles it. If you're overriding slippage on the `/order` call, consider whether your override is actually better than RTSE's estimate. In most cases, it isn't.

If you're using `/build`, invest time in dynamic slippage logic. Polling `getRecentPrioritizationFees` and recent swap outcomes for your pairs, then adjusting `slippageBps` before each build call. A static value will cost you money on one end or the other.

If you're building trigger orders, understand the default split between take-profit (RTSE) and stop-loss (20%). Override the stop-loss default only if you have a specific reason and understand that tighter slippage means higher failure risk during volatility.

And if you're debugging "my swaps keep failing" issues, check the slippage before checking the RPC. I've spent hours helping partners optimize their RPC setup when the real problem was 0.3% slippage on a volatile pair during peak hours.

## Why I wrote this

Slippage is one of those things that feels solved once you pick a number. Set it and forget it. But the right number changes every second, and the cost of being wrong flows in two directions: failures that frustrate users or MEV that drains value.

I used to think of slippage as a config value, like a timeout or a retry count. Something you tune once and leave. It's actually more like a real-time bidding system where you're constantly negotiating between execution certainty and price protection, and the market conditions that determine the optimal bid are always shifting.

RTSE isn't magic. It's a system that takes the inputs I was ignoring (congestion, liquidity, volatility, execution history) and does the math I wasn't doing. The result is something I should have built myself but never would have, because I didn't realize how much the static approach was costing me until I measured it.
