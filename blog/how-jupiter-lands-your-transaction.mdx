I used to call `sendTransaction`, wait three seconds, and check if it landed. About 30% of the time, it didn't. The transaction would just vanish. No error, no confirmation, no revert. Gone.

My first instinct was always "I need a better RPC." I upgraded providers, tried dedicated nodes, paid for priority endpoints. Landing rate went from maybe 70% to maybe 78%. Better, but not good. And every time the network got busy, it dropped right back down.

Then I started using the `/execute` endpoint on the Swap API, and my landing rate went to the high 90s. I assumed it was just a nicer RPC behind the scenes. It's not. It's a completely different approach to getting a transaction on-chain, and understanding the difference changed how I think about Solana execution.

---

## First guess

My assumption was straightforward: Jupiter has really good RPCs. Maybe they run their own validators, maybe they have premium Jito connections, maybe they just have a faster pipe to the leader. When you POST to `/execute`, it calls `sendTransaction` on a better RPC than the one I was using, and that's why it lands more often.

To test this, I ran a simple experiment. I took the same signed transaction bytes and sent them two ways: once through my own RPC using `sendTransaction`, and once through Jupiter's `/execute`. Same transaction, same signature, same blockhash.

Over 50 swaps during a moderately busy period:

| Method | Landed | Failed | Expired | Landing Rate |
|--------|--------|--------|---------|-------------|
| My RPC (`sendTransaction`) | 36 | 3 | 11 | 72% |
| Jupiter `/execute` | 48 | 1 | 1 | 96% |

The 11 expired transactions on my side were the giveaway. Those transactions were valid, correctly signed, had sufficient compute budget. They just didn't reach the leader in time. The blockhash expired before the transaction was processed.

That's not an RPC quality problem. That's a delivery infrastructure problem.

---

## What sendTransaction actually does

When you call `sendTransaction` on a standard RPC, here's what happens. Your RPC node receives the transaction bytes, forwards them to its connected validators, and those validators gossip it through the network toward the current leader. The leader might process it. Or the leader slot might end before the transaction arrives. Or the transaction might sit in a queue behind thousands of others with higher priority fees. Or the leader might be geographically far from your RPC's validator set.

You get back a transaction signature immediately, but that's just an acknowledgment that the RPC received it. Not that it was processed. Not that it reached the leader. Just that one node has it. Then you poll `getSignatureStatuses` in a loop and hope.

Most developers add retries on top of this. Send it, wait 2 seconds, check status, if not confirmed send it again. This helps, but it's duct tape. You're resending the same transaction through the same infrastructure that failed to land it the first time.

The failure modes are diverse and hard to diagnose:

| Failure Mode | Symptom | Your Typical Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| Blockhash expired | Transaction disappears after ~60s | Retry with new blockhash |
| Insufficient priority fee | Transaction stuck behind others | Increase fee, retry |
| Leader rotation | Tx arrives after slot change | Retry, hope for better timing |
| RPC congestion | Slow forwarding to validator | Try another RPC |
| Network congestion | Everyone's txs are delayed | Wait and retry |

Each failure mode requires a different response, but from the developer's perspective they all look the same: the transaction didn't land. So you retry blindly and hope the next attempt hits a different failure distribution.

---

## What Beam does instead

Jupiter Beam is the execution infrastructure behind `/execute`. When you POST a signed transaction to `/execute`, here's what happens under the hood.

**Priority fee optimization.** Beam doesn't use a static priority fee. It reads current network conditions, recent block inclusion rates at various fee levels, and the specific compute units your transaction needs, then sets a priority fee calibrated to land in the next few slots. During congestion, the fee goes up. During quiet periods, it goes down. You're always paying the minimum necessary for fast inclusion, not a hardcoded number that's either too high (wasting money) or too low (failing to land).

**Multi-path delivery.** Instead of sending through one RPC to one validator set, Beam sends your transaction through multiple RPC providers and direct validator connections simultaneously. The transaction races through several paths toward the leader. If one path is slow or congested, another one gets there. This alone eliminates most of the "expired blockhash" failures I was seeing.

**Confirmation polling.** After sending, Beam polls for confirmation aggressively and handles retries internally. If the first attempt doesn't confirm within a tight window, it retries with adjusted parameters. You don't manage any of this. You get back a response with a status.

**RTSE adjustment.** This one surprised me. RTSE, the Real-Time Slippage Estimator, can adjust the slippage tolerance on your swap during execution. If network conditions changed between when you got the `/order` response and when `/execute` fires the transaction, RTSE adapts. I wrote a [separate deep dive on RTSE](/blog/how-jupiter-knows-what-slippage-to-set) if you want the full picture.

The combination of these four systems is why the landing rate gap is so large. It's not one optimization. It's an entire pipeline replacing what was previously "send it and hope."

## The requestId link

One detail that's easy to overlook: the `requestId` field. When you call `/order`, the response includes a `requestId`. When you call `/execute`, you pass that same `requestId` along with your signed transaction.

This isn't just a logging correlation ID. The `requestId` links your quote to the execution context. Beam uses it to know which routing engine won the quote (Metis, JupiterZ, Dflow, OKX), what the expected output was, what slippage was estimated, and how to handle the execution.

For JupiterZ (RFQ) quotes specifically, this link is critical. When a market maker wins the quote through JupiterZ, the quoted price is a commitment from that market maker. During `/execute`, the market maker adds their signature to the transaction to fulfill their side of the trade. That's why the Swap API docs tell you to use `partiallySignTransaction` instead of `signTransaction` for `/order` responses. Your wallet signs first, then the market maker counter-signs during execution.

If you lose the `requestId` or try to execute a stale one, the quote context is gone and execution will fail.

```typescript
// Get the order
const orderResponse = await fetch(
  `https://api.jup.ag/swap/v2/order?inputMint=So11111111111111111111111111111111111111112&outputMint=EPjFWdd5AufqSSqeM2qN1xzybapC8G4wEGGkZwyTDt1v&amount=100000000&taker=${walletAddress}`
);
const order = await orderResponse.json();

// Sign the transaction (partially, for RFQ compatibility)
const transactionBytes = Buffer.from(order.transaction, "base64");
const transaction = VersionedTransaction.deserialize(transactionBytes);
transaction.sign([wallet]); // partial sign

// Execute with the linked requestId
const executeResponse = await fetch("https://api.jup.ag/swap/v2/execute", {
  method: "POST",
  headers: { "Content-Type": "application/json" },
  body: JSON.stringify({
    signedTransaction: Buffer.from(transaction.serialize()).toString("base64"),
    requestId: order.requestId,
  }),
});
```

The `requestId` ties the quote to the execution. Drop it and you break the chain.

## Error codes that actually tell you something

When `/execute` does fail, the error codes are structured into categories that tell you where in the pipeline things went wrong.

**Aggregator errors (-1000 to -1004):** Something went wrong during route computation or swap instruction building. The routing engine couldn't build a valid transaction. This usually means liquidity shifted between `/order` and `/execute`.

| Code | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| -1000 | General aggregator error |
| -1001 | Route computation failed |
| -1002 | Swap instruction build failed |
| -1003 | Simulation failed |
| -1004 | Compute unit estimation failed |

**RFQ errors (-2000 to -2004):** The market maker side of a JupiterZ quote failed. The market maker couldn't fulfill their commitment, timed out, or rejected the trade.

| Code | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| -2000 | General RFQ error |
| -2001 | Market maker rejected the fill |
| -2002 | Market maker timed out |
| -2003 | Market maker signature failed |
| -2004 | RFQ quote expired |

**Execute errors (-1 to -3):** The transaction was built and signed correctly but failed during on-chain execution.

| Code | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| -1 | Transaction expired (blockhash too old) |
| -2 | Transaction reverted on-chain |
| -3 | Unknown execution failure |

The category tells you whether to retry (execute errors), wait and retry (aggregator errors), or accept the failure (RFQ errors where the market maker backed out). Most developers I work with lump all errors together and retry blindly. These codes let you be smarter about it.

## Self-managed execution: what you're signing up for

If you use `/build` instead of `/order` + `/execute`, you get raw transaction instructions and handle execution yourself. Here's what that means in practice.

| Responsibility | `/execute` (Beam) | Self-managed (`/build`) |
|---------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Priority fee calculation | Automatic, adaptive | You calculate or hardcode |
| RPC selection | Multi-path delivery | Your single RPC |
| Retry logic | Built-in, intelligent | You build it |
| Confirmation polling | Handled internally | You poll and timeout |
| Slippage adjustment | RTSE at execution time | Static `slippageBps` |
| RFQ liquidity | Available (JupiterZ) | Not available |
| MEV protection | Included | You handle it |

Some teams need self-managed execution. If you're composing Jupiter swaps into larger transactions (liquidation bots, arbitrage, protocol-level operations), you need the raw instructions from `/build`. You can't add custom instructions to an `/order` transaction because the routing engine may return a partially-signed RFQ transaction that you can't modify.

But if you're building a swap interface, a portfolio rebalancer, a payment flow, anything where the swap is the primary operation, `/execute` handles infrastructure you'd otherwise spend weeks building and months maintaining.

I've seen teams build retry systems with exponential backoff, priority fee estimators that poll `getRecentPrioritizationFees`, multi-RPC failover with health checks, and confirmation polling with timeout management. That's real engineering time on infrastructure that isn't your product. Beam replaces all of it with one POST.

## The numbers that changed my mind

I ran a longer test over a weekend. 500 swaps of varying sizes on varying pairs, split between self-managed and Beam execution.

| Metric | Self-managed (my best setup) | Beam (`/execute`) |
|--------|-----------------------------|--------------------|
| Landing rate | 81.4% | 97.2% |
| Median confirmation time | 4.8s | 1.2s |
| P95 confirmation time | 18.3s | 3.1s |
| Avg priority fee (SOL) | 0.000089 | 0.000062 |
| Total retries needed | 143 | 0 (handled internally) |

The landing rate gap I expected. The confirmation time gap I didn't. Median 1.2 seconds versus 4.8 seconds means Beam isn't just landing more transactions. It's landing them faster.

And the priority fee line is interesting. My self-managed setup was paying more per transaction and still landing fewer of them. Beam's adaptive fee calculation was more efficient because it has better data on current network conditions than my `getRecentPrioritizationFees` polling.

Zero retries on my side with Beam. Not because nothing failed. But because when something failed internally, Beam handled the retry before I ever saw it.

## What this means for integrators

If you're using `/order`, always pair it with `/execute`. The two endpoints are designed as a pipeline. Using `/order` to get a quote and then sending the transaction yourself through `sendTransaction` means you get the routing benefits (all four engines) but lose the execution benefits (Beam, RTSE, multi-path delivery).

If you're seeing landing rates below 90% on self-managed execution, the fix isn't a better RPC. It's a better pipeline. Or just use `/execute`.

If you're debugging failed swaps, check the error code category before retrying. Aggregator errors (-1000s) mean the route went stale. RFQ errors (-2000s) mean a market maker bailed. Execute errors (-1 to -3) mean the on-chain submission failed. Different problems, different responses.

And if you're building on `/build` for composability, understand the execution trade-offs you're accepting. Budget engineering time for priority fees, retries, and confirmation management. Or better yet, build your custom logic around the swap and use a separate `/execute` call for the swap portion.

## Why I wrote this

For a long time I thought transaction landing was about having a fast connection to Solana. Better RPC, closer to the leader, more likely to land. That's true in a narrow sense, but it misses the bigger picture.

Landing a transaction reliably on Solana is a systems problem. Fee estimation, multi-path delivery, retry intelligence, slippage adaptation, confirmation management. Each of those is its own engineering challenge, and they interact with each other in ways that make the combined system much harder to build than any individual piece.

I spent weeks building retry logic for a project last year that `/execute` would have handled in one call. I wish I'd understood this sooner.
